Protocol Guild has deployed the latest [Agora](https://voteagora.com/) Governor smart contract, a bespoke membership NFT, and full Governance Stack, which is an evolution of the work Agora have done for over 15 other DAOs including Optimism, ENS, Uniswap, Ether.fi, Syndicate, Towns, Shape and Scroll. These changes have enabled us to manage our onchain funding contracts more effectively, in addition to letting the DAO control our legal entity.

This post explores: 

- Why Protocol Guild needed a DAO
- Our past, present and future usage of decentralized governance mechanisms

## Background

When Protocol Guild launched as a pilot in 2022, our goal was to become the gold standard for leveraging blockchains for public goods funding, including aiming to be a fully onchain organization. However, by the end of the pilot period, it became clear that a legal entity would be essential to scaling our fundraising efforts.

After consulting with various teams in the ecosystem, we deployed a legal entity in the Cayman Islands. This choice was predominantly due to the Cayman's supportive legal framework for non-profit foundations, specifically allowing foundations to be "memberless" (i.e. having no shareholders) and be controlled by a DAO.

This structure enabled us to create a legal entity capable of receiving and distributing funds, while still ensuring that a **DAO retained control** over everything. Beyond legal necessity, there was also an important technical reason to deploy a DAO: **reducing multisig dependencies**, specifically for our split contract.

As background, Protocol Guild's smart contract architecture has always included contracts built by the talented [Splits.org](https://splits.org/) team. This includes;

1. A 1 or 4 year **vesting contract** to receive donations (immutable, with no controller, forcing donations to vest).
2. A **"split" contract** to distribute vested tokens directly to a registry of Ethereum's core protocol contributors.

While the vesting contracts required no controller, the split contract needed one to allow for updates \- specifically, to remove inactive contributors and add new ones. At launch, this split contract was controlled by a Safe multisig because we did not yet have a DAO. However, our long-term plan was always to transfer ownership of this critical module to a DAO.

Thus, we needed a DAO for two primary reasons: to control our legal entity and to manage our onchain funding contracts. Below is a diagram of what our smart contract architecture looked like during our pilot, before we had a DAO.

## PG DAO V1: Moloch DAO

For our first DAO, we deployed an off-the-shelf implementation of **Moloch V3**. That name should be familiar to many in the Ethereum ecosystem \- “[MolochDAO](https://molochdao.com/)” aka “The Original Grant Giving DAO” was built on the same foundations. [DAOhaus](https://daohaus.club/), the devs behind the code, were thus a natural fit given their background in public goods funding as well as their battle-tested tooling.

Accordingly, in [April 2024](https://admin.daohaus.club/#/molochv3/0x1/0x412a32dd71357bd12337f4408168df903f90cbd3/settings), we summoned a Moloch V3 DAO comprising 172 of Ethereum's active core protocol contributors. The parameters were:

* **Voting Period:** 7 days
* **Grace Period:** 2 days
* **Voting Share:** One-member-one-vote
* **Approval Threshold:** 51%
* **Quorum:** 33%

The deployment of this DAO officially kicked off "Protocol Guild V2," i.e. our post-pilot implementation:

![Protocol Guild Smart Contract Architecture 2](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/protocolguild/protocol-guild-site/refs/heads/temp-blog/public/20260501-agora-dao/Protocol%20Guild%20Smart%20Contract%20Architecture%202.png)

Eagle-eyed observers will notice that the split contract was still controlled by a multisig, and not the Moloch DAO. Instead, the DAO was used to ratify changes to the membership registry onchain, which our multisig then used as a signal to update the split contract.

**Why didn't our Moloch DAO control the split directly?** Because the DAO’s high quorum requirements (at least 33% of the DAO had to vote for a proposal for it to pass), made it unfeasible to permission the split to the DAO \- we didn't want to impose the overhead of having to reach 33% quorum every time we wanted to trigger split distributions, which was ideally weekly. To be clear, the 33% quorum was our choice \- most DAO’s have a quorum of less than 5%\! \-  but we wanted to set the bar higher for ourselves, especially considering our DAO’s important place in our legal framework. DAOhaus could also have built out the functionality of having different proposals with different quorum, but out of an abundance of caution we prioritized using audited, off-the-shelf contracts without any custom code. (This caution stemmed from our own failed experience building a custom DAO, but more on that later.)

Overall, this still allowed us to achieve our goals of establishing a legal entity while enshrining onchain governance, and we are grateful to DAOhaus and our Moloch DAO for enabling that. You can learn more about DAOhaus [here](https://daohaus.club) and contact the team [here](https://discord.gg/kJaVkXtsXA). 

## PG DAO V2: Agora DAO

By late 2025, our Moloch DAO served us well for over a year and a half. We then gradually transitioned to a new DAO built by **Agora**. Although a newer project, Agora has already had great success [powering elements](https://www.agora.xyz/blogs/1-rpgf-retro) of Optimism’s Retroactive Public Goods Funding program, and serving as the governance layer for many of the biggest projects in our space. 

The central factor in our migration decision was Agora's **"scoped proposal type"** security feature. Scoped proposal types enable policies on contract functions, optionally arguments on those functions, to bind to quorum and approval thresholds. We required two distinct proposal types:

1. **Proposal Type 1: Membership Updates**
   1. **Quorum:** 33%
   2. **Approval Threshold:** 51%
   3. **Usage:** Updating the DAO and split contract
2. **Proposal Type 2: Distributions**
   1. **Quorum:** 0%
   2. **Approval Threshold:** 100%
   3. **Usage:** Weekly split (funding) distributions

Essentially, Agora allowed us to "scope" split distributions to the second proposal type (requiring no quorum) while scoping all other DAO and split functions to the first type (maintaining the 33% quorum). This enables control permissions for the governor, on a per proposal-type basis. This flexibility unblocked the migration of full control of the split contract to the DAO, achieving our goal of reducing multisig dependencies. We deployed [our Agora DAO](https://gov.protocolguild.org/) in September 2025 and sunset the Moloch DAO in Q1 2026\.

This migration also brought several new features and capabilities to our membership:

- Modern mobile-friendly UI
- An “abstain” voting option
- A “[notification hub](https://www.agora.xyz/blogs/9-notification-hub)” allowing DAO members to opt into receiving governance updates via email, Discord, Telegram and others
- First-class Safe multisig [support](https://www.agora.xyz/blogs/15-safe-wallet-improvements) with expedited workflows, allowing users to go straight to onchain proposals without drafting or signing in
- Features we haven't adopted but could explore in the future: [gasless voting](https://www.agora.xyz/blogs/4-gasless-voting-for-ens-and-uniswap) and [private voting](https://www.agora.xyz/blogs/12-private-voting)

Since this migration, we have distributed [over $2 million in value](https://dune.com/queries/7390513?sidebar=none) directly to Ethereum's core protocol contributors via our Agora DAO. We also built a [Dune dashboard](https://dune.com/protocolguild/protocol-guild-agora-dao) specifically to track DAO metrics. We are incredibly grateful to the entire team for their support. ## Future Plans

Despite all this progress, there’s still much more we’d like to do\! To become the gold standard for leveraging blockchains for public goods funding, Protocol Guild must continue upgrading its smart contract architecture to remove trusted components, manual inputs, and offchain dependencies.

To that end, we invested significant time in developing an [onchain membership registry in 2023](https://github.com/HausDAO/protocol-guild-contracts#protocol-guild---networked-member-registry-contracts--), which kept track of each recipient's [split share](https://protocol-guild.readthedocs.io/en/latest/01-membership.html#split-share) and leveraged state bridging to allow the mainnet DAO to control membership registries and split contracts on L2s. We even had a [work-in-progress UI](https://github.com/HausDAO/protocol-guild) for this\! Ultimately, we decided not to deploy those contracts after realizing the scope of our ambition would introduce excessive complexity and potential security risks. We want to once again thank [DAOhaus](https://daohaus.club) for all the incredible work they did on this, as well as to [Sigma Prime](https://sigmaprime.io/), [Dedaub](https://dedaub.com/), [Zellic](https://www.zellic.io/) and [Red Guild](https://theredguild.org/) for their audits.

But we're not done evolving. As new tools and solutions appear in this fast-moving space, we'll keep exploring them. Our modular architecture ensures we can adapt and implement changes efficiently. Expect more updates in the future as we iterate on our architecture and share our learnings along the way.
